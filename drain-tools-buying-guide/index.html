How to choose a drain tools

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose the right drain tools, start with a hair catcher drain cover to prevent clogs, then keep a manual drain snake (often called a hand auger) for when something still gets through. Match the snake’s cable length and thickness to your most troublesome drain, and pick a hair catcher that fits snugly and is easy to clean.

What’s the difference between a drain snake and a hair catcher drain cover?

A drain snake (or plumbing auger) is a flexible cable that you feed into a pipe to break up or retrieve a clog. It’s a reactive tool, you use it after a blockage has already formed. A hair catcher drain cover, on the other hand, sits over or inside the drain opening and physically traps hair and debris before they can travel down the pipe. It’s a preventive tool. Both serve different stages of drain maintenance. The hair catcher reduces how often you need to snake, while a snake is the brute-force option for when prevention fails or for deep clogs that a cover can’t stop. Many households benefit from having one of each on hand.

How do I pick the right drain snake for my home?

Drain snakes come in two broad styles: manual (hand-cranked) and electric (powered). For most household sinks, tubs, and showers, a manual snake with a cable that’s roughly a quarter-inch thick and 15 to 25 feet long is plenty. It’s affordable, easy to store, and gives you enough reach for standard P-traps and short pipe runs. Thicker cables are better for toilets and main lines, but they require more muscle and care. Electric snakes are overkill for routine bathroom clogs and can damage older pipes if you’re not experienced. Stick with a manual model unless you regularly battle stubborn tree roots or long horizontal runs. Look for a snake with a corrosion-resistant cable (often wound steel) and a comfortable handle grip. Some models have interchangeable tips, a hook end for grabbing hair, a bulb end for pushing through sludge.

What should I look for in a hair catcher drain cover?

Fit is the most critical thing. Hair catchers come in standard sizes (usually 1.5-inch or 2-inch openings for bathroom drains), but your drain flange might be slightly different. Measure the inside diameter of your current drain opening before buying. A loose cover lets hair slip past; an overly tight one is a pain to remove. Flexible silicone or rubber catchers conform better to irregular openings than rigid plastic or metal ones. Material matters for durability and cleaning. Silicone catchers are non-slip, easy to pop out, and can be washed in the dishwasher. Stainless steel options are more durable and look nicer but can be harder to clean because hair wraps around the mesh. Whichever you choose, look for a design with fine holes (small enough to catch hair but large enough to let water flow fast) and a smooth surface that won’t snag your fingers or nails.

Can a hair catcher and a drain snake work together?

Absolutely, in fact, that’s the most effective approach. A good hair catcher handles about 90% of the hair and goop heading down the drain, so you’ll snake far less often. But no cover is 100% foolproof. Tiny strands, soap scum, and stray debris can still accumulate over time, especially in showers and tubs with heavy use. When you do need to snake, the hair catcher makes the job easier because the clog is usually looser and closer to the surface. Just remove the catcher before feeding the snake in, and clean the catcher itself, hair wrapped around its prongs or mesh is often the real cause of the slow drain, not a deep blockage. Together, they keep drains flowing with minimal effort.

How often should I clean or replace my drain tools?

Hair catcher drain covers should be cleaned every time you notice water pooling in the sink or shower, which might be once a week in a busy bathroom. A quick rinse and a swipe with an old toothbrush is usually enough. If you see mold or mineral buildup, soak it in white vinegar for 20 minutes. Replace the catcher when the silicone tears, the mesh rusts, or it no longer fits snugly, about every year or two for most brands. Manual drain snakes are nearly indestructible. Rinse and dry the cable after each use, then apply a light coat of oil to prevent rust. The only part that wears out is the tip; some models let you replace it. If the cable kinks badly, it’s time for a new snake. Electric snakes require occasional cleaning of the drum and checking the cord for nicks, follow the manufacturer’s instructions for motor maintenance.

Frequently asked questions

Will a hair catcher stop all shower clogs?

No, but it will stop the vast majority caused by hair. Soap scum, toothpaste bits, and small objects can still cause slow drains. A good hair catcher reduces clogs by 80–90% in most households.

Can I use a drain snake on a clogged toilet?

Yes, but only with a closet auger, a specialized short snake with a rubber sleeve that protects the porcelain. A regular household drain snake can scratch or crack the toilet bowl. Always use a toilet-specific auger.

How do I know what size hair catcher to buy?

Measure the inside diameter of your drain opening at the top. Standard bathroom drains are usually 1.5 inches, but kitchen sinks and some tubs are 2 inches. Check your measurement against the product’s listed compatible sizes.

Is it safe to pour chemical drain cleaners down the sink if I use a snake?

No, never use chemical drain cleaners if you plan to snake. The chemicals can create toxic fumes or splash back when the snake penetrates the clog. They also damage the snake’s cable and can harm pipes. Stick to mechanical snaking.

Can a drain snake damage my pipes?

Yes, if used roughly or with the wrong cable. A thick, rigid snake in delicate PVC pipes can crack them. Always feed the cable gently and stop when you meet serious resistance. For old or fragile pipes, consider calling a plumber instead of forcing the snake.

How long does a typical hair catcher last before it needs replacing?

Most silicone or rubber catchers last 1 to 2 years with regular cleaning. Metal mesh catchers can last 2 to 4 years, but the hair buildup is harder to remove. Replace once the catcher loses its shape or the holes stretch out.

In shortStart with a well-fitting hair catcher in every shower, tub, and bathroom sink. Keep a manual drain snake as backup for the stubborn clogs that inevitably slip through. Clean the catcher weekly, snake only when water slows, and avoid harsh chemicals. That simple routine will keep your drains flowing smoothly for years with very little hassle.
